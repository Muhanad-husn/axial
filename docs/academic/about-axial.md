# What we're building, and why we're asking for your help

*Companion to `outreach-letter.md`. The letter is the thing you send. Keep this on
hand for the colleague who asks the natural follow-up: "how is this different from
just uploading the books to ChatGPT?" It draws that line explicitly, point by
point. Send it only if asked.*

## The short version

Axial is a research instrument for comparative-historical analysis. You give it a
*case* and a *question* (for example, "Syria, 2011–2024: how did the fragmentation
of the armed opposition shape the trajectory of state collapse?") and it produces
an original analytical answer, grounded in a curated library of scholarly sources,
with every claim attributed, opposing positions surfaced, and its own confidence
stated honestly.

It is not a chatbot and not a search engine. Think of it as a disciplined research
assistant that has read a specific shelf of serious books, coded every passage by
the kind of claim it makes, and can synthesize across them, while telling you what
it is inferring versus what a source actually said, and what it could not find.

## Why it is different from the tools you already know

You have probably tried uploading PDFs to ChatGPT or Claude and asking questions,
or used a "deep research" button. Those are useful. Axial is deliberately
different in ways that matter for scholarship.

**Against uploading sources to a chatbot (RAG).** A chatbot retrieves a few
relevant passages and paraphrases them. It answers *from* a source. It rarely
separates what a source *claims* from what the model is *inferring*, it does not
attribute reliably, and it will produce a fluent, confident paragraph whether or
not the sources support it. It never tells you what it left out. Axial's core
discipline is the opposite. Every claim in its answer is marked as either **(a)
what a specific source states** or **(b) the tool's own cross-source inference**.
A (b) claim with no real grounds is a failure the system is built to catch, not a
feature.

**Against hybrid RAG and knowledge-graph systems.** Those improve *retrieval*:
they find better passages. Axial's harder problem is *synthesis*, producing an
argument that no single source made, across sources, the way a scholar writes a
review essay, but held to the attribution a scholar is held to.

**Against "deep research" on the open web.** Those tools scrape whatever the web
offers, weigh a preprint the same as a blog post, and sometimes cite sources that
do not exist. Axial works only on a curated corpus of real scholarship and never
reaches outside it. Its grounding is structural: it cannot cite what is not in the
corpus.

## The honesty layer is the real point

Three things no consumer AI tool does, that a scholar would insist on:

- **Opposing positions are required.** If the corpus contains a serious challenge
  to the answer and the answer ignores it, that absence is flagged as a red flag,
  not passed off as a clean result.
- **Coverage is disclosed.** The answer shows which cases the evidence actually
  covered and where it was thin, so "we do not really know" stays visible instead
  of being papered over.
- **Confidence is calibrated and honest.** No manufactured "0.87" precision. Thin
  evidence is reported as thin.

The name comes from **axial coding**, the qualitative-research method of tagging a
corpus by concept and relating those concepts. That is literally the substrate:
every passage is coded by claim-type, field, empirical scope, theoretical school,
and its role in the argument, and the analysis runs over that coded structure.

## Why it matters

Comparative-historical synthesis across a large body of scholarship is exactly the
work that is too big for one person to hold in their head, and exactly the work
that today's AI does badly and confidently. A tool that does it with rigorous
attribution and honest limits would be a genuine research instrument rather than a
plausible-sounding toy. We are building it first on Syria scholarship, which is why
we are asking Syria scholars.

## What we are asking of you

Small, and it helps a great deal. A few good research questions from your area of
expertise. The details are in the accompanying note. Even three is a real
contribution, and nothing about this is urgent.
