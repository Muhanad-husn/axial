## The letter

Dear [NAME],

I am writing to ask for something small and specific: the research questions you would actually put to the thirty works listed at the end of this letter. Not questions invented to be helpful to me. The ones that come out of your own work. [Personalize: how you came to their work.]

I have built a piece of software that reads across those thirty works and tries to answer questions from them. I can check whether it stays honest. I cannot check whether its answers are any good. That is why I am writing to you rather than to a wider audience.

**What the thing is.** It is software, not a scholar. Given a question, it reads across the thirty works and assembles an answer out of what those books actually say. Every claim it makes points back to the specific passage it rests on. Every claim is also marked as one of three things: something a source states directly, something the software concluded by putting several sources together, or speculation that runs past what the books support. The middle category is where it is most likely to be wrong, so it is labelled rather than hidden. The system does not produce scholarship, and it is not meant to stand in for anyone who does.

**Why I need domain experts.** Whether the software stayed inside the books, and whether its labels are honest, are mechanical questions. I can test those and it passes or fails clearly. What I cannot test is whether an answer is any *good*: whether the question was worth asking in the first place, whether the argument would be taken seriously by someone in the field, whether it has walked past the obvious objection. That judgement requires knowing the literature. I do not have it. This is the actual gap, and no amount of further engineering closes it.

**What I am asking for, in three tiers.**

1. **The questions themselves.** Two or three research questions you would genuinely put to this literature. Rough phrasing is fine. This is the whole ask, and it takes about fifteen minutes.
2. **What a strong answer would have to establish.** If you have it: the things an answer would need to get right before you would credit it. What would make you dismiss an answer on sight.
3. **Where the answer should come from.** Which of the thirty works your question should rest on, and where the real scholarly disagreement sits on it.

Tier one on its own is valuable and sufficient. It is what I most need. Please do not feel you have to reach tier three or say nothing. [Optional: name a question area close to their work.]

**What happens to what you send.** Your questions become test cases. I run them through the system and record how it does. Nothing is published under your name, or with your name attached to it, without asking you first.

**What I can offer in return.** Acknowledgement wherever this work is written up, if you want it. The results the system produces on your questions, sent to you directly. And an honest account of where it failed on them, which on current evidence is the more interesting half. I am not going to promise anything beyond that, because I cannot yet verify that I could deliver it.

There is no deadline and nothing turns on a quick reply. If this is not worth your time, no answer is needed.

The source list follows.

With thanks,

[Sign-off]

## Notes for the founder — not part of the letter

Decide these before anything goes out. The first two are correctness problems; the rest are things a specialist reader will notice.

**1. Two entries are partial holdings and the appendix currently says so. Decide whether that is acceptable or whether to re-source them.**

- *Mann, volume 2* is 85 pages of a roughly 800-page volume, stopping mid-sentence in chapter 3. Volume 2 is the volume on classes and nation-states, which is the one most likely to be central to the questions these academics will send. Answering a state-formation question "from Mann" while holding a fifteenth of that volume is the kind of gap a specialist will spot immediately.
- *Üngör, Paramilitarism* is the introductory chapter only, twenty pages, with no title page. The author attribution is itself an inference from a self-citation in a footnote.

  Recommendation: re-source both before sending. If that is not possible, the appendix as drafted describes them accurately, which is the second-best option. What is not acceptable is listing either as a whole work.

**2. The Kandiah master's research paper.** An unpublished MSc research paper sits in this list next to Princeton, Cambridge, and Michigan monographs. Every recipient will notice, and some will read it as a signal about how the list was assembled. Two questions for you: should it stay in the reading list at all, and if it stays, does it need a sentence of justification in the letter? The appendix currently labels it accurately and says nothing more.

**3. The bibliography's own summary counts do not add up.** `docs/corpus-bibliography.md` says "24 of the 30 items are complete scholarly books" and "7 are edited collections", but the entries it then names give 6 edited collections (Beshara; Hall and Schroeder; Heydemann twice; Kao and Lust; Malešević and Haugaard). By my count of the entries: 27 of the 30 are books, of which 6 are edited collections and 1 (Mann volume 2) is partial; the remaining 3 are an article, a chapter, and the master's paper. I have used my count in the appendix. The bibliography's summary paragraph should be corrected to match before anyone reads both documents side by side.

**4. The Chouliaraki subtitle discrepancy is unresolved and the appendix says so.** This is honest but slightly odd in a list sent to academics, since the answer is a five-minute check against the publisher's catalogue. Worth resolving rather than shipping as an open question.

**5. Two forward-dated items.** Kao and Lust is dated 2025 and the Malešević article is dated *Theory and Society* 55, no. 10 (2026). Both are plausible as recent or forthcoming, but confirm the volume and issue numbers on the Malešević article before an expert on that literature checks them. A wrong citation on a very recent article in a specialist's own field is a costly error.

**6. Elcheroth and Reicher's publisher is inferred, not printed.** The appendix says the publisher is inferred from the series and identifiers. That is defensible, but it is a third flagged uncertainty in a list of thirty. Consider whether resolving items 4, 5, and 6 outright would make the list read as more carefully assembled than one carrying several visible unknowns.

**7. What the list implies about scope.** The list leans heavily toward Syria and the Levant, with a general comparative-historical spine (Mann, Tilly, Malešević, Wimmer, Kalyvas). A Middle East specialist may ask why there is nothing substantial on the Gulf, Egypt, Iran, or North Africa beyond the Kao and Lust collection. Not a defect, but be ready to say the scope is deliberate rather than accidental.

**8. Recipient-specific care.** Several living authors and editors on the list (Malešević, Heydemann, Bayat, Kao, Lust, Vignal, White, Wimmer, and others) work in exactly the fields you are writing to. If any recipient is an author or editor of a listed work, say so in the opening paragraph rather than let them find themselves in the appendix. That also changes the ask slightly: they are unusually well placed to say where the system misreads their own argument.
